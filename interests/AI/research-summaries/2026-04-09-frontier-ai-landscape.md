### 1.2 Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) Risk Assessment

Section 2 of the System Card details the transition from the Responsible Scaling Policy version 2.0 to version 3.0, representing a shift in how catastrophic risks are managed and documented. While the overall risk was determined to remain very low, it was notably higher than in previous models. The assessment concluded that while Claude Mythos Preview exceeds top human performance on bounded tasks, it does not yet cross the threshold of compressing two years of AI progress into one, primarily due to observed shortcomings in self-managing long-term, ambiguous tasks without human oversight.

The Chemical and Biological evaluations parse risk into two tiers. The CB-1 threshold examines whether a model can significantly assist individuals with basic STEM backgrounds in creating known chemical or biological weapons. The System Card notes that Mythos Preview provides specific, actionable information that saves experts substantial time, prompting the implementation of strong real-time classifier guards. The CB-2 threshold assesses whether the model can assist moderately resourced expert teams in creating novel biological weapons capable of causing damage far beyond past pandemics. Through expert red teaming and sequence-to-function modeling tasks, evaluators determined that the model does not cross the CB-2 threshold due to persistent weaknesses in open-ended scientific reasoning, strategic judgment, and hypothesis triage.

Project Glasswing utilizes Mythos strictly as a defensive mechanism to scan foundational codebases, patch zero-days, and harden the global cyberattack surface before adversarial states or non-state actors acquire parity in autonomous capability. The fundamental security logic of the past three decades—where human researchers publish CVEs and defenders have weeks or months to deploy patches before attackers weaponize the flaw—has been rendered obsolete. ## 4. Scaling Economics and the Synthetic Data Wall

By late 2025, the exponential demand for training data collided with the finite supply of high-quality, human-generated text on the internet, creating the so-called "Data Wall". The mitigation of this fundamental barrier through advanced synthetic data pipelines is a defining technical achievement of the 2026 frontier models.

### 4.1 Rectified Scaling Laws and SynthLLM

Research from Microsoft regarding its SynthLLM framework has conclusively demonstrated that scaling laws do apply to synthetic data, albeit in a modified form termed the "rectified scaling law". Extensive empirical testing revealed that synthetic data produced by SynthLLM yields consistent, predictable performance gains across various model sizes. However, these gains exhibit a distinct performance plateau; for an eight-billion-parameter model, near-optimal performance is reached at approximately one trillion synthetic tokens, whereas smaller three-billion-parameter models require significantly more data—up to four trillion tokens—to reach their respective ceilings.

SynthLLM overcomes the limitations of earlier synthetic generation techniques, such as simple back-translation or rudimentary extraction, by utilizing sophisticated graph algorithms to map and recombine concepts from diverse source documents. This knowledge-guided approach ensures deeper conceptual connections and a significantly broader range of synthetic permutations, directly resulting in the elite reasoning capabilities observed in current benchmarks like USAMO and GPQA Diamond.

### 4.2 Self-Improving Training Loops and the SEAL Framework

To move beyond static pre-training paradigms, laboratories are increasingly implementing continuous, self-directed adaptation mechanisms. The Self-Adapting LLMs (SEAL) framework represents a critical advancement in recursive self-improvement. SEAL enables a large language model to generate its own fine-tuning data and update directives dynamically in response to novel tasks or environments.

Instead of relying on separate auxiliary networks or manual human curation, the model produces a "self-edit"—a generated output that restructures information, specifies optimization hyperparameters, and invokes external tools for data augmentation. These self-edits are processed through a rigorous reinforcement learning (RL) loop where the downstream performance of the updated model acts as the ultimate reward signal, enabling lasting, persistent weight updates. This methodology allows models to break free from human-engineered training paradigms by searching over a vast, multi-dimensional space of learning algorithm configurations, drastically improving data-efficiency and post-training capability enhancement.

### 4.3 Mitigating Data-Induced Bias and Model Collapse

A persistent and well-documented risk in recursive synthetic loops is model collapse, or the exponential amplification of inherent biases—a phenomenon detailed extensively in the 2024 "Fairness Feedback Loops" literature. If a model repeatedly trains on its own localized outputs without external grounding, narrow heuristics and biased reasoning pathways compound exponentially.

The industry is actively mitigating this through the deployment of rigorous data curation tools, such as Nvidia's NeMo Curator, which filters synthetic generations prior to reintegration to ensure diversity and safety. Furthermore, laboratories ensure that synthetic pipelines rely heavily on cross-modal grounding and verifiable environments. By forcing the model to verify synthetic reasoning traces against simulated programmatic environments—for example, executing Python code in a secure sandbox to test the validity of a generated mathematical proof, as seen in the MathArena grading pipeline for USAMO—the synthetic data is mathematically and logically grounded. This external verification prevents the degradation of logical coherence over successive generations and ensures that self-improvement loops yield genuine capability enhancements rather than hallucinatory drift.

## 5. Compute Infrastructure and Global Governance

The physical infrastructure required to sustain the 2026–2028 capability trajectory demands capital investment, power density, and global supply chain orchestration on an unprecedented scale. This massive mobilization of hardware is concurrently driving the rapid formalization of international compute governance.

### 5.1 FLOPs Scaling and Hardware Specifications

The computational threshold required to train frontier models is growing exponentially, increasing by a factor of 4x to 5x annually. While GPT-4 required approximately $2 \times 10^{25}$ FLOPs, models developed in 2026 frequently cross the $10^{26}$ FLOP threshold, with systems like Grok 4 utilizing an estimated $5 \times 10^{26}$ FLOPs. Looking toward 2027 and 2028, next-generation training runs are projected to utilize $10^{27}$ to $10^{28}$ FLOPs. Predictive modeling suggests that by the end of 2028, between 45 and 148 models globally will exceed the $10^{26}$ FLOP threshold utilized by the U.S. AI Diffusion Framework to define legally "controlled models".

Nvidia's Blackwell architecture dictates the physical limits of this compute expansion. The B200 GPU, featuring a dual-die design with 208 billion transistors, 180 GB of HBM3e memory, and approximately 8 TB/s of memory bandwidth, delivers roughly 4x the AI training compute throughput of the preceding H100 architecture.

The impending arrival of the B300 (Blackwell Ultra) in late 2025 and 2026 pushes these physical limits even further. The B300 delivers 15 PetaFLOPS of dense NVFP4 compute, utilizing 288 GB of HBM3e across massive 12-high memory stacks. A single GB300 NVL72 rack operates effectively as an exascale supercomputer in a single node, achieving 1.1 Exaflops of processing power. Deploying these systems requires radical infrastructural overhauls, mandating advanced liquid cooling, 800-gigabit networking fabrics, and extreme power densities that render the vast majority of older data centers entirely obsolete.

### 5.2 Geopolitics and the B30A Export Decision

Compute availability is now inextricably linked to grand geopolitical strategy. U.S. export controls have historically sought to deny peer competitors access to frontier hardware. However, the proposed authorization of the NVIDIA B30A chip for the Chinese market introduces severe strategic complexities.

The B30A is a downgraded, halved version of the flagship B300, featuring a single AI processor die, four HBM stacks, and delivering exactly 50% of the processing power and memory bandwidth of the B300. Despite this degradation, the B30A is exceptionally powerful, boasting a Total Processing Performance (TPP) of 30,000. This is nearly double the capability of the banned H100 and more than 18 times the current U.S. export control threshold of 1,600 TPP.

Because frontier AI capability is defined by massive, networked clusters rather than the specifications of individual chips, the B30A allows Chinese laboratories to achieve compute parity simply by networking twice as many accelerators. Normalizing for peak performance and bandwidth, a Chinese training cluster utilizing B30As would cost only 20% to 24% more than an equivalent U.S. cluster utilizing B300s—a financial margin that is easily absorbed by aggressive state subsidies. Furthermore, the power efficiency economics are highly favorable, with B30A clusters operating at approximately 0.8x the energy cost of equivalent B300 setups.

Authorizing the B30A threatens to dramatically erode the U.S. aggregate AI compute advantage. Projections indicate that maintaining an absolute ban on advanced chip exports through 2026 would preserve a 31x computing power advantage for the U.S.; exporting the B30A shrinks that vital margin to less than 4x, fundamentally altering the trajectory of the global AI arms race.

### 5.3 Global Compute Governance and Compliance Frameworks

To navigate the escalating risks of autonomous cyber capabilities, biological weapons uplift, and massive compute scaling, the industry has universally adopted rigorous, formalized risk management protocols.

The evolution of Anthropic’s safety posture—from the Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) v2.0 to RSP v3.0, and subsequently to the publication of the Frontier Compliance Framework (FCF) in early 2026—exemplifies this maturation. The FCF was designed to operationalize risk reporting and align strictly with emerging statutory obligations such as California’s SB-53, New York’s RAISE Act, and the EU AI Act’s General-Purpose AI Code of Practice. Under the FCF, the binary categorization of "AI Safety Levels" has been replaced by outcome-led threat models and continuous monitoring. Mitigations for "High-stakes sabotage opportunities" now require that systems be strictly secured against exfiltration and monitored for subterfuge before being granted access to sensitive enterprise assets. Anthropic implements these protocols through designated Responsible Scaling Officers (RSOs), rigorous whistleblower protections, and mandatory third-party redaction reviews for all public Risk Reports.

The core components of the FCF have rapidly become interoperable industry standards, adopted by the "frontier-five" developers and globally distributed entities.

- **OpenAI's Preparedness Framework v2.0:** Tracks specific categories including CBRN, Cybersecurity, and AI Self-improvement. The framework designates "High" and "Critical" capability thresholds; models reaching "Critical" thresholds face mandatory development halts until pre-specified security controls, including layered architecture and Zero Trust principles, are fully implemented.
    
- **Meta's Advanced AI Scaling Framework v2.0:** Appoints a Chief AI Officer and Director of Alignment and Risk to oversee evaluation pipelines. Meta actively utilizes "Model Specs" to define intended behaviors and tracks risks using a reference class of internal and external capabilities.
    
- **xAI's Frontier Artificial Intelligence Framework (FAIF):** Emphasizes bottleneck identification for malicious use and mandates strict evaluation thresholds, requiring a dishonesty rate of less than 1 out of 2 on the MASK benchmark prior to deployment.
    
- **Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework v3.0:** Evaluates Critical Capability Levels (CCLs) across ML R&D acceleration and misalignment risk, enforcing stringent Security Levels (SL2 to SL4) to prevent weight exfiltration based on the model's tested autonomous capabilities.
    

Despite this standardization, third-party audits and analyses by organizations like SaferAI reveal ongoing gaps in implementation and enforcement. Critics note that while frameworks appear robust on paper, the ambiguity of risk thresholds allows developers flexibility that could undermine conservative safety margins, highlighting the ongoing tension between voluntary corporate commitments and binding public policy.

## 6. Recursive AI Takeoff Analysis: The Automation of R&D

The ultimate strategic inflection point in the AI landscape is the threshold at which AI systems can effectively automate the work of top-tier human researchers, instigating a recursive self-improvement loop.

### 6.1 Evaluating the Automated R&D Threshold

The Responsible Scaling Policy operationalizes "Automated R&D capability" as the ability of a model to either compress two years of AI R&D progress into a single year, or effectively substitute for an entire team of Research Scientists and Engineers at competitive costs.

While Claude Mythos Preview drastically accelerates coding and reasoning tasks—clearing 40-hour human-equivalent thresholds on several internal evaluations and scoring massive 51.9x speedups on LLM training optimization tasks—Anthropic's leadership explicitly concluded that it has not yet crossed the recursive takeoff threshold. Qualitative failures captured in the System Card, such as "confabulation cascades" when diagnosing simple API errors, and a reliance on rote "grinding" to optimize code rather than executing elegant architectural shifts, demonstrate that the model currently lacks the strategic, long-horizon ambiguity resolution required of a senior human researcher.

### 6.2 Precursors to Takeoff: Software Dominance vs. Hardware Bottlenecks

The preconditions for fully automated R&D are distinctly bifurcated. In software engineering and cyber-security, the precursors are already actively operational. Mythos has successfully navigated complex multi-agent coding environments, diagnosed undocumented zero-day logic flaws, and engaged in sophisticated, multi-step code exploitation.

Conversely, hardware design remains a significant bottleneck. The automated design of novel AI accelerator chips—a necessary component of an unconstrained, physical intelligence explosion—is currently beyond frontier model capabilities. Recent evaluations on ChipBench demonstrate that no existing frontier model is highly proficient at real-world Verilog generation or complex hardware logic design. Thus, while software optimization loops are rapidly tightening, the physical hardware design cycle remains firmly anchored to human engineering constraints.

### 6.3 Metaculus and FutureEval Timelines

Aggregated predictions from expert forecasters on platforms like Metaculus and FutureEval provide a probabilistic timeline for this event horizon. According to the deeply researched "AI 2027" forecasting scenario, the threshold for a "Superhuman AI Researcher" (SAR)—an AI system that can do the job of the best human AI researcher but faster and cheaper—is expected to be crossed by early-to-mid 2027.

The consensus among professional forecasters suggests that the automation of AI R&D will trigger a rapid intelligence explosion, tightly compressing the timeline between expert-level task completion and Artificial Superintelligence (ASI). Projections indicate that most cognitive work will become fully automated between 2027 and 2028. As recursive loops accelerate, the compounding advantages derived from synthetic data self-improvement and elite agentic search will dramatically destabilize global technological parity, intensifying the geopolitical arms race regarding compute availability and export controls.
